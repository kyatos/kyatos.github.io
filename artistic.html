vision & focus


My first medium was poetry. It gave me a form which could tolerate and, I discovered, even lovingly house the fragmented psychic states of my childhood. It continues to form the basis of my aesthetic tastes and artistic approaches.

𓈓

I like to write and make work which reaches towards pre-verbal states of consciousness. My aesthetic language often revolves around poetic images, structures, and approaches, leading to dreamscapes which blend reality with absurd or surreal conditions; motifs and metaphors which transmute the human body into animals, cryptids, mythological creatures, and cyborgs; and a persistent attraction towards repetition, variations on a theme, and failure.

My work is traumatophilic. That is also why my process is slow, especially if other people are involved. Walking towards a wound together takes care, awareness, trust, and an appetite for risk.

The moving body is often present as another language and carrier of personal and intergenerational histories, which I use to sidestep and meander away from cognitive intellect. I am fond of "untrained" movers, especially those who are willing to move towards honesty with/ through their body.

𓈓

I consider the two key parts of my artistic responsibility to be:

(a) To nurture, sustain, and follow the trail of my artistic intuition and gift to its ends; and

(b) To use this artistic gift to cultivate alternative possibilities of what the world could be -- in imaginative, speculative, and material ways.

A future piece, which I hope to grow into when I reach my 50s, is to aid others in unblocking and expressing their artistic intuitions; to serve as a mentor or guide in their journeys.

𓈓

I generally feel that artistic content has developed more aggressively than artistic form, and that the gap between the two is where most artistic energy should be used to expand what art can be. Without further formal maturation, content becomes a poor simulation of what it aspires to be.

That being said, I also believe that content and form are inherently entwined bodies, and cannot entirely be considered independently of each other. To understand how to develop a form, one must also address the kinds of content it has been made for.

𓈓

I am generally medium-agnostic in my approach to artistic projects, in that I look for the ideal genres, mediums, and forms which will best serve the artistic process and goals of a project, without taking any medium as my default.

However, I do gravitate towards poetry, speculative fiction, performance texts, and performance work in general as my most effective artistic languages.

I am generally known as a writer of poetry, and am now also focused on directing interdisciplinary performances under the banner of theatre.




principles & practices


  1. Commitment and consistency produce the best conditions for experimentation, deviation, and meaningful chaos.
  2. The early stages of an artistic process are like a cross between a murder investigation and a religious pilgrimage. One must follow the scent of their intuition down numerous trails with blind trust, without demanding clarity and coherence. The larger picture often surfaces much later, when enough clues have been gathered for a pattern to emerge.
  3. Reject nothing. Then reject something. Then reject something else. Narrow the field of vision slowly; never rush to marry a single coordinate.
  4. It's more important to know what you won't do than what you will do.
  5. But when you're doing something, it's more important to define what you ARE doing than what you're NOT doing.
  6. The natural rhythm of one's artistic process and production is commonly confused, obscured, and disrupted by capitalistic work practices and structures. As much as possible, one must seek to revitalize, nurture, and protect this rhythm. Space and time are needed for this rhythm to feel safe enough to re-/emerge.
  7. This rhythm may not look like that of other artists', much less those with overglorified hyper-productive, hyper-disciplined routines.

    You have to define for yourself what your cycles look like and what they comprise of. They might not involve public presentation. They might have long fallow periods. They might be extremely brief and extremely intense, igniting only once a year. They might plod along at a steady pace for years with no end in sight.
  8. Your artistic practice has a life of its own. You may not be the kind of artist you think you want to or should be. You may be able to delude other people and yourself about the kind of artist you are, but the artistic voices channelling through you will not be deceived.
  9. Along that vein, I'd argue that much of your artistic practice is not about who you are, though it borrows upon your person and intentions to come into existence.
  10. Therefore your job is not to judge, critique, or force the work into being a certain way. Your task is to observe keenly, notice what the animal that is the work needs and is capable of, and to create the ideal conditions for it to arrive fully and flourish. You are a conscious vessel, facilitator, and midwife.
  11. Your artistic practice will prepare you to be its vessel by first offering you healing and pleasure through creativity and expression. Use and relish it.

    There will come a time when you no longer need the art to stem the blood or keep the wound open/closed. You will be ready then for expressions beyond selfhood and ego to arrive! You will begin to create things far beyond you. These processes are nonlinear and may take place concurrently (healing yourself and channelling beyond yourself).
  12. If you are a control freak, release some of it by developing access to your unconscious/ intuition and keeping it fed with trust, appreciation, and a delicious artistic diet. When you do so, you can relegate more thinking, sensing, and decision-making to it. Simply absorb, trust, sleep on it, and let the answer arrive like a sudden knowing.
