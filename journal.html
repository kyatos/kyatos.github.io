Once, I was tasked with visiting a solo exhibition and covering it via Instagram Stories for a publication I was working part-time for. Not used to engaging with visual art, I found the work rather elusive and opaque; yet it also endeared itself to me. Traces of the artist could be felt on the work, his patient hands and simplicity of heart. I did my best with the Story coverage, although I felt like a fish out of water as I filmed and refilmed clips, and fussed over the little bits of text I added to each Story (What to say? What font to use? How to position it on the Story in "cool" way?).

Shortly after, I received messages and then a call from my editor. She said the work by the artist was terrible, and I should have informed her and known better than to post anything about it. I had been given zero say in the editorial decisions of the publication up to this point. She scolded me, and I took down the Stories. I felt sorry for the artist even though he had no idea any of this had transpired. I decided I did in fact like his work.

Over the next 4-5 years, I left the publication (after a drawn-out struggle involving emotional blackmail from the editor). The editor met with a series of painful life circumstances and had to go reinvent herself elsewhere. Somehow, I encountered an even more erratic and cruel editor at another publication who resorted to intimidation and legal threats. I had to grow even stronger, and despite the sensitivity that renders me at times so fragile, I did-- with thanks to my family and friends.

More importantly, I came to share many mutual acquaintances and friends with the artist of the solo exhibition. I can confirm now, without the motive force of spite, that I do truly like his work. It is honest and meticulous-- that's why it is beautiful.

We conduct acts of terror upon each other, not only in large-scale wars but also in our interpersonal relationships. Is it true that how you do one thing is how you do everything? The parts of me that like the sense of control I get from essentializing things to predictable patterns like this aphorism. Pithy and deployable. The protective parts of me are also my violent parts.

My practice of nonviolence has been selective and strategic, because to force myself to be totally anything feels itself violent and brutal. I am overly fond of discipline. I am overly permissive of ill discipline. My impulse now is to collapse both statements into moderation; something compassionate, fluid, amorphous. I resist it. Now there is no landing site for me in my use of the English language. This homelessness might be a step towards deeper alienation, on the other side of which is an indescribable belonging. I sense this position lying past the horizon of what I can see. Whether it is my will or not, I have a domain there. I dislike the word coming to mind but I will admit what it is: I have a 'kingdom' there.

After nearly 30 years of losing, grieving, and restoring an innocent part of my nature, I am now maturing with it. As though returning to an early Save point in the game to continue what was derailed before. Perhaps that is merely an easy narrative structure to use for interpretation, or a narrative structure which provides the interpretation I subconsciously most want. Nonetheless: my view of violence has become muddled.

Firstly, as Anne Bogart declared in her book A Director Prepares, I have come to agree that making theatre that is "good" demands forcefulness, a kind of violence in deciding, asserting, and fixing a vision in place. As I practice being firmer and more assertive about my standards, I find myself having to have more "violent" conversations-- I have to say things straight and tell them what I have decided. Sure, there are the conversations and discussions and the careful listening and responding. But at the heart of it, I have a responsibility to myself and the context (of work, or romance, or whatever else) to put my foot down.

Secondly, people who are too sane, who have no forceful qualities erupting from them despite the sanitization of our lives by structural conditions-- trouble me. I sense illness within them: their souls are asleep, or badly drugged, or disfigured. They can proceed linearly with their lives knowing that their comforts rest upon the humiliation and exploitation of other lives. They've bound or mutilated the parts of them that see.

Thirdly, as I told a friend: while I believe that any acts of violence harm the actors as much as the recipients, and that every act of violence, however seemingly justified, only perpetuates an ever-growing volume of itself-- I would find it hard to begrudge a victim of violence the act of retaliation and self-defense they may deeply desire. Could I be such a betrayer of the individual, coaxing them to sacrifice themselves for some collective ideal that might not arrive in their lifetime, which I cannot even be sure that we as a species are capable of achieving?

Am I convinced that we can become a spiritually-mature species? Even if not, what remains a meaningful function of believing in a spiritually-mature humanity? It makes me more open to people, more compassionate, and more willing to help others. It makes me more focused on the spiritual development of all human beings.

As a counterpoint, I wonder about the left-wing arguments I've read which critique beliefs that appear revolutionary but in fact make you more passive and nondisruptive , even more productive, within violent systemic conditions and authorities. How does one meaningfully define those terms: passive, nondisruptive, productive. Is it as black and white as: if you aren't against them then you're for them? Or: if you aren't moving then you're still? If I sincerely enjoy work and labour, but unfortunately enjoy them within a capitalist system, have I betrayed the anti-capitalist cause? I hesitate to agree with the dualistic mind fucks which the English language encourages within left-wing Overton windows.1 I have used much time in them and think I have to manoeuvre into other pathways.

I wonder how much of the misery of anglophonic philosophers is derived from thinking in this deeply limited language.

About violence: I have no conclusions. Except that force is a necessary component of effective relationship and existence. Where force gives way to violence, I'm not sure.

1For one, why do we welcome positions along a spectrum for expressions such as gender and sexuality but not racism? Also, violence and nonviolence appear to me to be more like fraternal twins than archenemies.
