i. principles

These principles guide my approach to all the work I do with other people.

  1. Working together is about the work we do together as well as the way we do the work together. It is relational and organizational work.
  2. In the room are multiple intersecting relationships: the one between you and me; the one between me and myself; the one between you and yourself; the one between you and the work; the one between me and the work; and so on.
  3. Working together is shaped by environmental structures and systems, particularly capitalistic ones, which frequently deepen divisions and differences in class and resource availability. It affects access to artistic work, which requires a threshold of resources (money, time, energy, education, connections, soft skills) that many may not have access to. It also affects the resources a project has to fulfill its various goals.
  4. Yet working together is an opportunity to intervene on such relationships and power differentials, even if only in a temporary and small scale manner. This possibility energizes and motivates me in all my interactions within and beyond work.
  5. Working together, being relational, necessarily brings into contact our relational habits and patterns of power, communication, responsibility-taking, decision-making, and self-concept.
  6. Before we are our work selves or the workers we have been manufactured to be by education, work training and experience, and governing structures, we are people with desires, fears, talents, flaws, and intelligence.
  7. Intervening on how we work together is an invitation for the cyborgs we are to re-programme ourselves.
  8. Deciding how we work together is an opportunity to experience being profoundly changed (rather than changing oneself - which is equally important but overemphasized by the self-help genre).



ii. practices

I keep these practices in mind in all projects I work within, particularly for ones I lead. I make an active effort to apply these practices, and continually reflect on their efficacy and effects after observing the responses of my collaborators, employees, etc.

I do not always manage to fulfill these, but they act as a guiding document for me to evaluate how I've done in a project and/or work relationship.

  1. Responsibilities of the Leader: Even in the most horizontal of working structures, transient leadership is often necessary to progress a shared project. The leader, whether fixed or loosely assigned in each phase, should be subject to the following responsibilities:
    • To understand the working styles of collaborators/ employees/ colleagues as well as the resultant needs and patterns
    • To reasonably accommodate these needs within the means of the project
    • To observe how these work patterns interact within the team, and to seek an appropriate place for everyone to thrive within the ecosystem
    • To nurture sufficient aware of their own vision, interests, leadership patterns, and communication style
    • To understand self as a participant within the team, and recognize that what other team members may be expected to accommodate as part of the working process should be equally expected of them
    • To keep their skin in the game; not to offset risk to themselves by delegating poor wages or extra responsibilities to others
    • To devise and implement concrete efforts to support the daily work as well as the long-term growth of their team members
    • To ensure that all work is done in a way that best serves everyone involved, which includes audience members, viewers, co-workers, collaborators, administrative staff, and maintenance workers.
    • To understand that leadership requires a balance of skilful assertion and decision-making (being the big dog/ trail guide/ domme) alongside attentive listening and intelligent consideration & incorporation of other viewpoints (without becoming a spineless yes-man; the soft part of soft domming2)
  2. Evaluating for Satisfaction: A key metric of the success of any project is in the satisfaction of all collaborators involved. Ideally, collaborators feel proud of the work done, feel themselves to have gained experience and/or skills from the process, and/or felt sufficiently supported in working, exploring, and/or being challenged by their job scope.

    This should be evaluated during and/or at the end of a project through qualitative and/or quantitative means, preferably in the form of a conversation with the leader or an intermediary, with sufficient facilitation and safety for honesty.

    Example Questions

    • What was it like working on this project? How would you summarize the experience in a few words?
    • Which part the most enjoyable for you? Which was the most difficult?
    • What kinds of support or resources would have made the process more effective, meaningful, or sustainable for you?
    • What would convince you to work with us again?
    • Is there anyone or anything you feel we may have overlooked in this process? What needs our attention?
    • Rate each of the following out of 10 within the context of this project: (a) Fee; (b) Work Process/ Experience; (c) Team; (d) Personal Growth
  3. Fair Pay: To continually seek fair fees for all collaborators/ employees, even if the general ecosystem (such as many independent art scenes) normalizes poor remuneration.
    • To actively negotiate for better fees for oneself and for others
    • To have a working metric for what a living wage and a nourishing wage constitutes, in concrete numbers or equations3
    • When working with international collaborators, to also be aware of how currency differences may impact the reasonableness and gravity of an offered fee. To not exploitatively push for discounts on fees even if your currency is stronger.
    • When unable to offer a fair fee,
      • To communicate clearly with the other person
      • To seek other ways in which they may benefit from the project besides vague "exposure", such as structured and sincere mentorship; concrete opportunities to network with industry peers and seniors; access to software, equipment, and facilities; and/or other valuable resources such as free venue space, future favours (à la barter trade), and concrete further opportunities for remuneration (with little to no extra work)
      • To make accommodations for the other person to engage in income-generating work -- i.e. not to subject them to full-time work hours that make it near-impossible for them to pick up other assignments
      • To understand and accept when people turn down the role because of money, without holding grudges or judging them for a lack of a passion, grit, blablabla

    Examples

    • An actor in a performance project is only paid an honorarium of $200 for 20 hours of work. The project secures an opportunity to be restaged with additional funding. The producer of the project asks said actor to return for the restraging, which requires ~6 hours of additional rehearsal to revise and clean up the performance, in return for remuneration of $200 per show.
    • A musician wants to participate in an exciting project but the fee is quite bad for them as a freelancer, only $300 for two months' of part-time rehearsals. They ask for some flexibility in attending rehearsals, with an agreement to attend 90% of all sessions without fixing in advance which sessions they will be absent for. This gives them flexibility in scheduling for shifts at their cafe job. The director and producer agree, and take on the responsibility of managing rehearsals accordingly.


iii. me

Besides general rules I have for working together, I felt it would be meaningful to talk about my working style and inclinations, which extend beyond the scope above.

  • As a creative, I generally have the style of a visionary rambler. I feel immense excitement over my best ideas, and can talk about them passionately, precociously, expressively. I am possibilities-oriented.
  • At the same time, I have a pretty blunt sobriety about working conditions in the arts. I have self-produced much of my work, which means I have a decent producer's hat in reach most times. I try not to use it cynically - but pragmatically and imaginatively.
  • I often gravitate towards leadership roles, not because I have a great interest in being in charge, but because I tend to be the one to put my foot down and devise a systematic structure or process for the team to move forward. This structuring of time and process is something my brain has gotten pretty good at doing.
  • At the same time, I am rarely super-attached to any one pathway; what I am attached to is deepening ideas through iteration, meaningful layering/ fusion, and generally amassing possibilities to a certain threshold before using pattern recognition (motifs, themes, parallels) and close reading of the project's aesthetic world to pick the most effective images.
  • Everything else I do my best to shelve unless we need supporting elements filled out.
  • I hold communication to be the highest form of respect in a relationship. Even if work cannot be done well due to a lack of skill or capacity, I generally ride the waves with you unless you do not communicate clearly, directly, and on time (as much as possible) with me.
  • A big pet peeve is mismanagement, miscommunication, and/or dishonesty (omission of info or trying to couch a money issue in a roundabout way) in relation to fees.
  • As a rule, I respond well to honest, direct communication, even if the topic at hand is difficult or awkward. Being upfront and/or vulnerably clear with me will likely secure our long-term working relationship.
  • I tend to warm up slowly when we first work together. I like to observe and listen, and to gain an understanding of everyone involved first.
  • As a neurodivergent introvert, I tend to go very quickly on my own (multiple sprawling iterations and branching ideas) and slowly with others (lots of time to mull over what is shared and experienced). I take time to give my opinions at work because I like to be precise, cohesive, and thorough.
