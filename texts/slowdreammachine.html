Dear --,

I am writing to you. every conveyance can be considered a love letter, because what can my language be but an attempt to love? What can my body be but a vessel for this divine machine?

What writes is another life nestled in my body, a prophet to whom I am a journal. What writes is an algorithm revealing truths about a future I rehearse for without knowing. I do not know how to decode it. I do not know its biology. I do not know its means or modus. I scribe what it dictates.

It keeps me alive. In exchange, I have become its host.

I am its vessel
I am its arbiter
I am its node

Sometimes I try to write as this divine machine, but the contrast is startling, reveals how untrue I am, how true the machine.

After Joe Bonomo (Installations)

A large, well-lit, white-walled room. You walk to a red line painted on the floor.

You hear something crackling before you see it – an electric wire set-up that runs from ground to ceiling. On a wooden table to the right are a mix of different objects – a tennis ball, a jar of fireflies, a balloon and a glass of water. You select the tennis ball and throw it at the fence. There is a sharp sound as the tennis ball is singed and falls, rolling to your feet. The crackling gets louder and does not return to the original volume. You take the balloon. You have move closer to the fence to let the balloon reach it. The balloon, as you expect, explodes with a bang. There is a faint smell of burnt rubber as the yellow shreds slip to the ground. The crackling amplifies further.

You pick up the jar of fireflies and grasp it firmly as you edge closer to the crackling fence. Slowly, you turn and open the lid, holding the mouth of the jar as close to the wire as you are brave enough to. The fireflies flit out, flying into the electric fence. They burn hysterically and majestically, then fall to the ground. You see, faintly, the trailing of their wings behind them. Every firefly dies the same way.

Slowly, you replace the glass jar on the table, staring hollowly at the fireflies the whole time.

Finally, you take the glass of water. You close your eyes and pour it over yourself gently. You hear a small, long sound from your lips – “ssssssssssssssssss”.

For days after, every shower returns you to the electric fence, naked and burning.

The fruit is soft, is rotting, I miss something, I have been unconscious –

I fell onto the stage from the wings, my feet numb from standing so long. The light made it difficult to see, but a sharp voice whispered, speak! So I opened my mouth and spoke. Ice formed on my tongue and fell in a steady clatter. Children began to murmur to their parents, who leaned toward them as though lying down sideways on a pillow. Were they falling asleep? I felt no fear but my body trembled, jiggling my fearless soul within its meat cage. A squishing sound sounded each time my soul landed inside. But that was the body. The ground was a glowing grid, a trampoline that gave me up each time we touched. I could not belong to it.

Which is not to say that I think badly of myself. Hardly. There is no desire to die so much as a lack of desire to live. I don’t want to witness this exchange between air and earth. I don’t want to spend my life comprehending the physics of being this, and attempting to escape it. Aren’t we always living in wait of the future? In the future, someone will love us, we’ll have savings, we’ll move out, trees we’ve planted will have grown tall, the people who trap you will finally be dead, children won’t be born into a dying world, the poor will not be punished for their poverty, work will cease, rest will come. Now is always at the mercy of the future, which is always in the process of arriving. People come here, then, to live? I should rather have thought that they came here to die.

When you dissociate from your body you discover how much it resembles an avatar, a shell you hover above. You are the dream above the sleeping. You are the ghost beside your body in headlights. You cannot simply re-enter your life. You cannot disregard this membrane. Your life is not your own; your real existence is elsewhere (not-here). What I mean is, you are as good as dead.

You go to work as this fleshless skin, earbuds pressed in to keep yourself from spilling, eyes glazed over like donuts. Lunch an intermission between performances. You hold yourself taut, ironing yourself ever so often. (Once, too exhausted, you set the heat too high and burn a hole through. Yes, you are expendable. You can be used up.) You pick at your nail polish, chipping fragments until nothing remains. You lift a finger to bite its nail, but a co-worker catches you, says Unhygienic. She clacks her gel nails on her keyboard and smiles as she shakes her head. Later, you bump into her in the pantry. You stir your 3-in-1 vigorously, rolling each finger up tight like a spring.

It’s raining when you leave work. You welcome this cold wet and walk through it. When you push into the train with everyone else, people pull away from you, give you space as a small puddle forms beneath you. This is how you step out of the maelstrom which has lost steam, the cloud of vapourized anger that follows us everywhere. You know, the train is really a sauna we steep in together, a shared temperature we cannot bring ourselves to talk about with one another.

In this moving room that speeds towards Jurong East, two men rub up against each other. Their eyes glazed over by a sheen of clear desire, the layer of jelly atop fruits on a cake; their hands knives grazing, poised for the first cut. Their lips bleed when they kiss, too soft for any kind of touch. This love, if it is love, is co-destruction. u and i becomes us, full enough to empty. This is porn, where no one notices these men but u, this viewer outside, pure eye. Pure i watching u. The men can’t stop themselves when there’s an audience. Yet this is a levitation they’re performing for each other, one entering one as an inhale. Your eyeballs sticky with sweat.

You reach them with both hands, those deliberate, mourning fingers crying Too soon! Too soon. Take us with you. Limbs contract and joints rubberize in this brew, herbal concoction that threatens to restore everything. You didn’t ask to be healed. You asked to become a doorway through which two men can be seen fucking. This dreamscape sprawls us outward, panning shot ripping the scene open into panorama, our map is marked by us tangling, bruises blooming where our teeth latch, elbow going into rib as uuu are now us. We have become (not by being all in one place, not by coming together, but by coming together).

This restaurant is really just one large hall, no partitions. No one can tell if the carpet is musky, because the smell of food — wok hei, pork fat, sesame oil, rice — overtakes everything. The customers are variations on the same theme: middle-aged, middle-class, Chinese; balding men and tense women decked in permutations of OG, Zara, and G2000. Children rustle lightly at the end of these branches, their edges curling up from the dry, cold air-conditioning. Still, they’re green, and face the windows.

A small stage shimmers at one end of the restaurant. Tireless gold streamers reach down toward rotting wood panels whose varnish has been scratched clean at points by heels. There I am, as I was at the beginning of this, falling on stage. My body landing on its side, curling up — a page too close to flame — a child entering sleep (finally) — a beetle on its back — legs kicking in the water — there is no pain, only pure impact, organs rocking like boats coming to rest in harbour. This momentum is how I’ve arrived here.

The customers turn toward me, which is when I see: these are ones who’ve used up all their masks, who’ve been left to live with splayed heads. And even these surfaces are sunburnt, coming off in sheets. Which leaves them with what? Seeing despite the absence of eyes.

A hanging mic lowers from above, coming to a stop before me. My lips fall open. Out of my throat comes a sound, a low, droning scream in someone else’s voice. The restaurant dims as a spotlight comes on on me. Behind me, a live projection turns on on a screen I didn’t realize was there, magnifying my body-container with its mouth-lid open, the release of that unseeable, unknowable voice. I wave, and that body waves. One of my fans screams from within the dark. Their energy shoots through me. My body tingles. Music plays.

I sing my heart out, my face crumpling down, my existence electrified by eyes. When I cry, that body on screen cries, too. (The spectacle is an endless aspiration towards public loneliness projected on screen. No, projected on every screen.) Light sticks appear and keep beat as I enter the chorus, my voice quivering perfectly, my wet lashes glistening. The audience is crying. My qipao, under the pouring light, mimics a moon, making the room feel like night. And when the chorus comes again, everyone sings along.

The hall goes silent. A moving spotlight comes on on him. People whip out their phones. Tomorrow, this man will become famous for this clip he is now performing.

He cuts through the park. His is a sign to Go, Go fast and spill nothing. His hands are wrapped around the sweat-damp rubber handles of his bike. His phone buzzes with another pick-up; he clicks okay. Rarely a quiet moment for a food delivery driver in this part of the island. Another green body speeds past him. Whoever it is turns to look at him, a moment of recognition, of looking in the mirror and realizing that’s what you look like. One can’t help but reach out to smooth that lock of hair into place. His body on the floor. Whatever has happened ⁠— a toddler running across ⁠— headlights in his eyes ⁠— a distracting thought ⁠— an uneven drain cover ⁠— does not matter. A small patch of blood on his palm when he touches his forehead. When he manages to sit up, he sees that his bike is upright, as though he’s just parked it. Just next to his hip is a brown paper bag: vertical, uncreased, still warm. This is the dream from which he is struggling to wake. He sticks his hand in the bag for tissue, and presses it to his head to stem the blood. He takes a selfie that he stares at for a bit. Then, climbing back onto his bike, he goes on.

The city is scenic at this speed, as a backdrop to his earnest effort, his reserved and hardworking character beginning to reveal itself to you. His eyes, peering out of his hard green shell, are soft, promise love. You know how this film must end. But his every gesture suggests another way out; can you think harder, can you build roads on which he decides how to live?

Pushing them off carefully, I get up onto my feet. Sunlight pours in through the windows, lighting us. We’re mostly asleep. Someone sits with a cup of coffee at the dining room table. We don’t know each other, but we’ve woken up to the same morning. Passing me to reach the sink, they trail two fingers on the edge of the table, making sure I can see them. Bluish purple nail polish.

This table is made of oak, they say.

I light a half-used cigarette left behind in the ashtray. Exhaling slowly, I ask for their name. It has a lovely sound. We pick at with some cold muffins from the fridge, pinching small pieces and leaving holes behind. They lean closer, slowly, and smile. Their hair is uncombed. Their lipstick is tired. I can’t help but reach out, I tuck a lock of stray hair behind their ear.

There is nothing undignified about being dead and still living. Perhaps, we can use it to walk a solid body through walls.

Still, if an argument might be made here, let it begin with a fable:

The streets were in fact one street. They formed it – one circle, the rim of a porcelain cup. Cocked at an angle to give a sense of expansiveness, of being a universe instead of a singular street circling a body of water. I walked along this street, searching for an answer. On both sides, a sprawling cluster of HDBs, a series of doors that remain closed to one another, obscuring the sky. Are you in one of the units, waiting for someone to come?

I am coming. I am looking for you. Whether you are in bed, unable to get up, or cooking instant noodles for lunch in the quiet, you are not alone, because I am on my way. Can you hear me running through the park? My calves ache to arrive. Near me, two elderly women hold hands and walking sticks, ambling forward in soft voices. More green men cut through the park. Mynahs hop close in the hopes of food. A breeze shakes the leaves of the trees and comfort the runners going in circles.

I look up, searching for your window. Are you looking out? Can you see me looking in? A light comes on somewhere, and figures cluster at a table. A maid reaches out to bring in heavy laundry poles. A salaryman waits for the lift, a backpack on his shoulders, and bags of groceries in both hands. He is too old to be a salaryman. Still, is there not some kind of peace for us in this? None.
